What Does an AI Consultant Actually Do?

Peter Skotte · February 16, 2026 · 5 min read

"AI consultant" sounds vague. Maybe a little buzzwordy. Fair. It's one of those titles that could mean anything from "I set up ChatGPT for your team" to "I built a custom machine learning pipeline that predicts your inventory needs six weeks out."

The range is wide because the field is wide. But the work itself? It's pretty concrete. Here's what it actually looks like, step by step.

Discovery: Learning Your Business

Every engagement starts the same way: we shut up and listen.

Not to a slide deck. Not to a pitch about what you think you need. We watch how your team actually works. We sit with the people doing the day-to-day -- the operations manager toggling between four spreadsheets, the sales lead manually copying data from one system to another, the customer service rep answering the same question for the 200th time this month.

We're looking for specific things. Where are the bottlenecks? What takes too long? What decisions are being made without data? Where does human effort get wasted on tasks a machine could handle?

This phase isn't about selling you anything. It's about understanding your business well enough to know what would actually help. Sometimes the answer surprises both of us.

Solution Design: Simplest Thing That Works

Here's something most people don't expect to hear from an AI consultant: not every problem needs AI.

Sometimes the answer is a Python script that runs every morning and pulls your data into one place. Sometimes it's a dashboard that shows your team what's actually happening instead of what they think is happening. Sometimes it's reworking a process so the expensive AI solution becomes unnecessary.

When AI is the right answer, we design the simplest version that solves the problem. Not the most impressive version. Not the version that would look good in a case study. The version that works reliably and your team can actually use.

This means choosing the right tools for the job. Off-the-shelf where it makes sense. Custom where it doesn't. We'll lay out the options, the tradeoffs, the costs, and the timeline -- and let you decide. You can see the kinds of AI solutions we typically work with.

Building: The Person Who Designed It Builds It

This is where a lot of consulting firms lose the plot. They send in a senior person for the sales meeting, then hand the actual work to a team of juniors you've never met. The solution that gets delivered barely resembles what was promised.

We don't do that. The person who designed the solution builds it. Custom integrations, automation scripts, AI models, dashboards -- whatever the design calls for. This matters because the person writing the code understands the business context. They were in the room during discovery. They know why decisions were made, not just what the spec says.

The build phase involves writing code, yes. But it also involves constant communication. You'll see working demos early and often. If something isn't right, we catch it while it's cheap to fix -- not after three months of heads-down development.

Deployment and Training: Making It Stick

A solution that works on a developer's laptop but never makes it into your team's daily workflow is worthless. Deployment is where the rubber meets the road.

This means getting the system into your actual environment. Connecting it to your existing tools. Making sure it performs under real conditions with real data, not the clean test data we used during development.

Then comes training. Not a 90-page user manual nobody reads. Hands-on sessions with your team, focused on the workflows they'll actually use. We document what matters -- the stuff people need to reference later -- and skip the rest. The goal is confidence. Your team should feel like this is their tool, not some black box an outside consultant dropped on their desk.

Ongoing Support: Things Change

Here's the thing about AI systems and automations: they're not "set it and forget it." AI models drift as data patterns change. Automations break when a vendor updates their API. A process that worked perfectly for six months needs adjusting because the business grew.

Good consulting doesn't end at deployment. We monitor performance, tweak parameters, fix what breaks, and expand what works. When your team has questions three months later, we're available. When you want to tackle the next problem, we already understand your systems.

This isn't about creating dependency. It's about acknowledging that technology lives in a changing environment, and someone needs to keep an eye on it.

When Should You Hire an AI Consultant?

Not every business needs one. But here are the signs that it's worth a conversation:

  • You're drowning in manual work. Your team spends hours on repetitive tasks that feel like they should be automated. Data entry, report generation, copy-pasting between systems -- the kind of work that's necessary but mind-numbing.
  • You have data but don't use it. There's a goldmine of information sitting in your CRM, your sales records, your customer feedback -- but nobody has time to analyze it. Decisions get made on gut feeling because the data is too messy or scattered to be useful.
  • Your competitors are moving faster. You're seeing other companies in your space shipping faster, responding quicker, and operating leaner. They might not be smarter -- they might just have better tools.
  • You've tried and failed to implement AI yourself. Maybe you bought a tool and it didn't stick. Maybe someone on your team tried to build something and it stalled. That's not failure -- that's normal. AI implementation is its own skill set, separate from the AI technology itself.

If any of those sound familiar, the right next step isn't buying software. It's having a conversation about what's actually going on and whether there's a practical solution.
